\section{Introduction}
Seconds to minutes after the Big Bang, the universe created the first nuclei~\cite{nasa:universe-overview}. These were primarily Hydrogen and Helium, with trace amounts of heavier elements~\cite{gorgen:presentations}. It was only a few hundred million years later that the first stars formed~\cite{nasa:universe-overview}, creating the heat and pressure necessary for nuclear fusion to occur. At this point, we know how stars produced elements up to iron through the fusion processes. The creation of heavier elements, however, is not sufficiently understood. The two leading theories for the production of these heavy elements are the slow neutron capture process (s-process) and the rapid neutron capture process (r-process). We also have intermediate process, and the proton capture process (p-process). All these processes play a part in the distribution of elements in the universe we see today. They all form a complex network of nuclear reactions, in which advanced nuclear reaction codes like TALYS \cite{TALYS} is essential to reproduce and understand astronomical observations.
 
\section{Nuclear physics and nuclear astrophysics}
Using astrophysical observations of absorption and emission spectra, we can determine the elemental composition of stars. This gives us insight into their abundance leading to necessary constraints in our modeling of their reaction rates. To understand this process we must learn how the elements are created in stars through nucleosynthesis, how this process changes over the lifetime of a star and how this leads to the production of the heavier elements.  


\subsection{Elements and their production in the Solar System}
Our previous model of how the sun emitted light was based chemical reactions, as we were used to seeing on earth, like the burning of coal. This model was not sufficient as it would not be possible for the sun to burn longer than approximately a few thousand years~\cite{gorgen:presentations}.

Jean Perrin realized in 1919 that the mass of the Helium atom was less then that of four Hydrogen atoms~\cite{gorgen:presentations}. Using Einstein's mass-energy equivalence, he proposed that the fusion of Hydrogen into Helium could provide enough energy to power the sun for billions of years. This set the stage for our modern understanding of stellar nucleosynthesis. 



\subsection{Nuclear Burning stages of stars}
\subsubsection{Hydrogen Burning}
The first stage of nuclear burning in stars is naturally Hydrogen burning, creating Helium-4. This is at first done through the \textit{pp-chain}~\cite{gorgen:presentations}. 

\paragraph{pp-chain:}\mbox{}\\
Writing out the full set of reactions in the ppI-chain:
\begin{align}
    {}^{1}\text{H} + {}^{1}\text{H} &\to {}^{2}\text{H} + e^{+} + \nu_{e},\\
    {}^{2}\text{H} + {}^{1}\text{H} &\to {}^{3}\text{He} + \gamma.
\end{align}
The reaction is then repeated and we get:
\begin{equation}
    {}^{3}\text{He} + {}^{3}\text{He} \to {}^{4}\text{He} + 2\,{}^{1}\text{H}.
\end{equation}
In total we can write the entire pp-chain as one reaction:
\begin{equation}\label{eq: pp-chain}
  4\,{}^{1}\text{H} \to {}^{4}\text{He} + 2e^{+} + 2\nu_{e} + 2\gamma.
\end{equation}
The creation of Helium-4 releases 26.7 MeV of energy, which is what powers the star. This is the first step in the pp-chain and we see Helium-4 as the most abundant Helium isotope in the universe. There is also the ppII and ppIII chains, producing Beryllium-8 and more $α$-particles. 

\paragraph{CNO cycle:}\mbox{}\\
With high enough temperatures we can also produce Helium-4 with the \textit{CNO cycle}. This dominates at temperatures of approximately 16 million Kelvin, just above the core temperature of the sun. This is due to triple-$α$ reactions needing high energies to overcome the Coulomb barrier of three $α$-particles. Through the Hoyle state in Carbon-12, which luckily corresponds to the excess energy from combining Beryllium-8 and Helium-4, we can briefly create Carbon-12 which then might de-excites to its ground, creating the Carbon we need.  

For this cycle Carbon and Nitrogen acts as catalysts to produce Helium-4 from Hydrogen. The Carbon is made from triple-$α$ reactions, which uses the Beryllium-8 created in the ppII and ppIII-chains, together with Helium-4 to create Carbon-12. The Carbon absorbs a proton, creating Nitrogen-13~\cref{CNO_1}. Nitrogen-13 goes through $\beta^{+}$ decay, creating Carbon-13~\cref{CNO_2}. The Carbon-13 then absorbs another proton, creating Nitrogen-14~\cref{CNO_3}. Nitrogen-14 absorbs another proton, creating Oxygen-15~\cref{CNO_4}. Oxygen-15 goes through $\beta^{+}$ decay, creating Nitrogen-15~\cref{CNO_5}. Finally, Nitrogen-15 absorbs another proton, creating back Carbon-12 and Helium-4~\cref{CNO_6} leaving us back at~\cref{CNO_1}. 

\begin{align}
    {}^{12}\text{C} + {}^{1}\text{H} &→ {}^{13}\text{N} + γ,\label{CNO_1}\\ 
    {}^{13}\text{N} &→ {}^{13}\text{C} + e{}^{+} + \nu_{e},\label{CNO_2} \\
    {}^{13}\text{C} + {}^{1}\text{H} &→ {}^{14}\text{N} + γ,\label{CNO_3} \\
    {}^{14}\text{N} + {}^{1}\text{H} &→ {}^{15}\text{O} + γ,\label{CNO_4} \\
    {}^{15}\text{O} &→ {}^{15}\text{N} + e{}^{+} + \nu_{e},\label{CNO_5} \\
    {}^{15}\text{N} + {}^{1}\text{H} &→ {}^{12}\text{C} + {}^{4}\text{He}. \label{CNO_6}
\end{align}

Alternatively, the Nitrogen-15 can also absorb a proton and create Oxygen-16~\cref{CNO_7}, which then absorbs another proton to create Fluorine-17~\cref{CNO_8} followed by a $\beta^{+}$ decay to Oxygen-17~\cref{CNO_9}. This finally absorbs a proton to create back Nitrogen-14 and Helium-4~\cref{CNO_10}, leaving us back at~\cref{CNO_4}.

\begin{align}
    {}^{15}\text{N} + {}^{1}\text{H} &→ {}^{16}\text{O} + γ,\label{CNO_7} \\
    {}^{16}\text{O} + {}^{1}\text{H} &→ {}^{17}\text{F} + γ,\label{CNO_8} \\
    {}^{17}\text{F} &→ {}^{17}\text{O} + e{}^{+} + \nu_{e},\label{CNO_9} \\
    {}^{17}\text{O} + {}^{1}\text{H} &→ {}^{14}\text{N} + {}^{4}\text{He}. \label{CNO_10}
\end{align}

After exhausting the Hydrogen in the core, there is no radiation pressure to counteract gravity, and the core starts to contract. As the core temperature increases, Hydrogen burning continues in a shell around the core. The luminosity increases, the star expands and cools, becoming a red giant. This increases the temperature and pressure, allowing for the next phase: Helium burning. 

\subsubsection{Helium Burning}
At around 300 million Kelvin, Helium burning starts in the core. The core expands and we see an increase in luminosity and surface temperature. The star begins to move towards the horizontal branch. The CNO cycle is now active in a shell around the core. 

\subsection{The production of heavy nuclei}
After the Helium in the core is exhausted, the core contracts again, increasing the temperature and pressure. Helium burning continues in a shell around the core, with Hydrogen burning in a shell further out. The star expands further and cools, moving up the asymptotic giant branch (AGB). A similar process continues for heavier elements. After Carbon burning, we have Neon, Oxygen and Silicon burning stages, each occurring at higher temperatures and shorter timescales, as seen in~\cref{fig: star_burning_stages}. This continues until we reach Iron-56, which has the highest binding energy per nucleon. Beyond this point, fusion no longer releases energy, and the star can no longer support itself against gravitational collapse. After quickly contracting the star expels its outer layers in a supernova explosion, leaving behind three possible remnants depending on the initial mass of the star: a white dwarf, a neutron star, or a black hole.

Beyond Iron-56, we need to look at neutron capture processes to create heavier elements. The two main processes are the slow neutron capture process (s-process) and the rapid neutron capture process (r-process). There is also the proton capture process (p-process) for creating certain proton-rich isotopes. These processes occur in different astrophysical environments and contribute to the abundance of heavy elements in the universe.


\paragraph{s-process}\mbox{}\\
The slow neutron capture process (s-process) occurs in environments with relatively low neutron fluxes. As it is so slow, it only produces stable isotopes along the valley of stability as seen in~\cref{fig: Resonance}. It slowly increases in mass number, until it steps of the line of stability. As the neutron capture is slow compared to the beta decay, the nucleus decays diagonally up to the left in the nuclide chart through $\beta^{-}$ decay. When low/intermediate mass stars in the AGB phase experience thermal pulses, they free neutrons through various reactions. This is over timescales of tens of thousands of years, explaining the low neutron flux, and why only a slow, but steady process could utilize these neutrons. For more massive stars, the neutrons are produced during Helium burning in the core. This process creates isotopes with mass numbers in range of approximately 60 to 90. 

\paragraph{r-process}\mbox{}\\
The rapid neutron capture process (r-process) occurs in environments with extremely high neutron fluxes, such as during supernova explosions or neutron star mergers. In these environments, nuclei can capture neutrons much faster than they can undergo beta decay. This leads to the creation of very neutron-rich isotopes, which then $β^{+}$-decay back towards stability after the neutron flux subsides. As this process occurs so fast and its condition are usually short-lived, we need to understand the cross sections of neutron-rich isotopes far from stability to model this process accurately. The r-process is responsible for producing approximately half of the elements heavier than Iron, including many of the heaviest elements like naturally occurring Uranium. 

\paragraph{p-process}\mbox{}\\
The proton capture process (p-process) happens much less frequently than the s- and r-processes. As we observe certain proton-rich isotopes that cannot be created through neutron capture and subsequent beta decay, we need another process to explain their existence. The p-process is thought to occur during supernova explosions, but is not yet fully understood. 


These processes all create a complex network of nuclear reactions, which influence the likelihood of each other happening. As seen in~\cref{fig: Resonance}, the number of pathways to create the different isotopes are complex and intertwined. Small changes in one reaction rate can have significant effects on the final abundances of elements produced.

\section{Nuclear Level Density \& Gamma-ray Strength Function}
\subsection{Nuclear Level Density}
When investigating lower excited states of a nucleus, we can mostly treat them as discrete energy levels. At higher excitation energies, however, the number of energy levels increases rapidly, and they become so closely spaced that we can no longer resolve them individually. This is not only due to there being more energy states closer to each other, but also due to higher excitation energies having smaller mean lifetimes $τ$, leading to a larger width $Γ = 1/τ$. This also contributes to the levels overlapping and becoming unresolvable. We therefore describe the \textit{density} $ρ$ of the number of energy levels $ΔN$, in a given energy interval $ΔE$ as a function of excitation energy $E_x$. 

\begin{equation}\label{eq: level_density}
  ρ(E_x) = \frac{ΔN(E_x)}{ΔE_x}.
\end{equation}

The level spacing $D = 1/ρ$ gives us the average distance between the energy levels at a given excitation energy. At lower excitation energies, we have $D ≫ Γ$, allowing us to resolve the individual energy levels experimentally. As we increase the excitation energy, $D$ decreases while $Γ$ increases. We enter into the quasi-continuum region when $D ≥ Γ$. In this region we cannot resolve the individual energy levels experimentally, but they are still separate. Finally, at even higher excitation energies, we reach the continuum region where $D ≤ Γ$. Here the energy levels overlap significantly, and we can no longer treat them as separate levels.

\subsection{Gamma-ray Strength Function}
The gamma-ray strength function (GSF) describes the transition probability of gamma-ray emission or absorption in $(n, γ)$ and $(γ, n)$ reactions respectively~\cite{utsunomiya:2020_gamma}. This is used in the Hauser-Feshbach model of compound nuclear reactions~\cite{PhysRev.87.366}. As it models both absorption and emission, it is split in two parts: The upwards and downwards GSF. 

\subsubsection{Downwards GSF}
The downwards GSF $\overleftarrow{f_{X_1}}(E_{γ})$ describes the probability of gamma-ray emission from an excited state in which $E_γ$ is the energy of the emitted gamma-ray and $X$ is either an electric (E) or magnetic (M) transition of multipolarity $1$. We assume from Weisskopf-estimates of gamma decay that a multipolarity of $1$ dominates the gamma decay~\cite{sunniva:presentations}. The function can be defined as follows~\cite{utsunomiya:2020_gamma}:

\begin{equation}\label{eq: DGSF}
  \overleftarrow{f_{X_1}}(E_{γ}) = \frac{\left<Γ_{X_1}(E_{γ} / E^3_{γ})\right>}{D_{ℓ}}
\end{equation}
where $<Γ_{X_1}>$ is the average width of the gamma transition and $D_{ℓ}$ is the level spacing of levels for s-waves or p-waves with $ℓ = 0,1$, respectively.

\subsubsection{Upwards GSF}    
The upwards GSF $\overrightarrow{f_{X_1}}(E_{γ})$ describes the probability of gamma-ray absorption. It can be defined as follows~\cite{utsunomiya:2020_gamma}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq: UGSF}
  \overrightarrow{f_{X_1}}(E_{γ}) = \frac{\left<σ_{X_1}(E_{γ})\right>}{3E_{γ}(πℏc)^2}
\end{equation}
where $<σ_{X_1}(E_{γ})>$ is the average cross section for gamma-ray absorption of energy $E_{γ}$. 

\subsection{Finding the GSF and NLD Experimentally}
Using the Oslo method and inverse kinematics, we can experimentally derive both the GSF and NLD for a given nucleus. This was done in 2020 using $d(\ce{^{86}Kr},pγ)\ce{^{87}Kr}$ reactions~\cite{Ingeberg2020}.

\section{TALYS}
TALYS~\cite{TALYS} is a nuclear reaction code designed to calculate important observables such as cross sections and reaction rates. It serves as an essential tool in exploring nuclear reactions such as the s-process and r-process in astrophysics. It is in constant development, with new versions being released regularly to included new and updated models. As of version 1.96, TALYS includes several models for both the gamma-ray strength function (GSF) listed in ~\cref{tab:gsf_models} and the nuclear level density (NLD) listed in~\cref{tab:ld_models}. With the use of the optical model model, in describes a plethora of nuclear scattering and absorption reactions. One can input your own experimentally derived models for both GSF and NLD if desired.

With a simple text input file, the blazingly fast fortran code can simulate nuclear reactions in an energy range from 0 to 200 MeV in mere minutes. As it is open source, it has many collaborators in the field constantly improving and updating the code with their research.

\section{Conclusion and Outlook}
Through this project, we have demonstrated the versatility and utility of the TALYS nuclear reaction code in simulating neutron capture reactions relevant to astrophysical processes. We saw how important the choice of model for the Oslo model potential is for accurately predicting reaction cross sections and rates, with models varying significantly in their results. It's easy to just try all models, but this is inefficient, time consuming and can lead to a locally optimal solution which might not work that well for other isotopes. Benchmarking against experimental data is therefore crucial to validate the models used. Through the use of experimentally determined gamma-ray strength functions and nuclear level densities, we attempt to bridge the gap between theoretical predictions and observed data even more. In this case there was not too much difference between the experimental and theoretical models when looking at cross sections, but this might not always be the case. Future work needs to systematically look at the assumptions of all the different models used in TALYS to see if there is any patterns to explain which models work and which do not.
